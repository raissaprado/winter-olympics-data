Introduction

The growth of women by the Winter Olympics Organization

While we wait for Corona to let us have Beijing’s Winter Olympics, let us check how far women go in the history of this multi-sport event. On this website we will analyze data from 1924 to 2014 olympic winners in order to show you the girl power.

Overall, the majority of men winners over women is undeniable. The total of medals given is decided beforehand by the olympics organization, so the data hereby analyzed reflects only on the point of view of the organization and not the achievement of each gender.

Women X sports

The ‘right sport’ to be a women

Once upon a time, in 1924, the far white land of Chamonix (France) would host the first Winter Olympics. Among 258 athletes, only 11 were women, and 6 went back home with a Skating medal. That was one small step for men, one giant leap for womankind.

Three editions later, in 1936, women would conquer the first medal in other sport than skating: skiing awarded 3 women and 27 men that year. Due to World War II, the next Winter Olympics happened only in 1948 at St Moritz, Switzerland. This year had 77 women athletes, resulting from 15 medals and Canada’s first women bringing a medal home.

It took time for women to have their moment to shine. Did they catch up and had more space in other sports?

As we can see, Skating and Skiing are by far the main sport for women, but not for men.

Location, location, location

In 1988 happened the Calgary’s Winter Olympic, where the story from Cool Runnings occurred. If you haven't seen this film yet, don’t blame me for telling some spoilers: Jamaica’s bobsleigh team didn’t win that year, or any of the years that followed. Despite the engaging story, we can’t be surprised with that fact, after all, they don’t even have the appropriate weather to train for it.

The event from 1992 was called by many as remarkable: Germany was back (after its unification), and South Africa was back after a thirty-two-year banishment. Let’s put aside all the political drama and focus on our girls: 72 medalists, Germany in the spotlight with 16 women in the podium, followed by the United States with 12. The proud women in me salute the proud women in you (although both of us have nothing to do with this achievement).
